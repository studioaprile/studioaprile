Material Recording System

A forensic protocol for documenting material behaviour under constraint

Material Recording is a method for producing, observing and describing how wood behaves under constraint, intervention and time. It treats the object as a changing condition rather than a finished form. Each piece is defined by geometry, timber and initial conditions, then subjected to pressure, exposure and intervention so that response can be observed rather than predicted. Cracking, rupture, deformation and collapse are not treated as defects but as points of legibility, revealing where material resistance ends and behaviour becomes visible. Objects are recorded over time as active, stable or resolved, forming a structured account of transformation rather than a fixed outcome. The work sits between sculpture, research and documentation, constructing conditions in which material can register its behaviour with precision.

Abstract

The Material Recording System (MRS) is a structured methodology for observing, documenting, and classifying the behaviour of natural materials, primarily wood, under controlled constraint over time.

Rather than treating objects as finished artefacts, the system treats each piece as an active record of moisture loss, internal stress, deformation, fracture, rupture, or collapse. The aim is not aesthetic optimisation but repeatable observation. Each object functions as a measurable instance within a broader body of records, enabling comparison across species, origin, geometry, defects, and drying conditions.

MRS operates at the intersection of craft, material investigation, and archival practice. It is a recording system rather than a production method.

1. Context and Intent

In most craft and sculptural traditions, material instability is corrected, concealed, or stabilised in order to preserve form. The Material Recording System reverses that logic.

Within MRS, instability is not treated as an error to eliminate but as the primary condition to observe. Deformation is preserved rather than corrected. Failure is classified rather than avoided. Time is treated as part of the work, not an external threat to it.

The system shifts the role of the maker away from authorship understood as control and towards authorship understood as the design of conditions. The resulting object is not simply made. It is recorded as evidence of a material process unfolding under constraint.

2. System Definition

The Material Recording System consists of three integrated layers.

2.1 Physical Layer

The physical layer concerns the object as produced under defined conditions. This includes material species, origin, moisture content, geometry, wall thickness, grain orientation, turning strategy, and visible defects such as pith, crotch, bark inclusion, fungal decay, insect activity, or pitch crack.

2.2 Temporal Layer

The temporal layer concerns the behaviour of the object over time. This includes drying progression, dimensional distortion, crack initiation, crack propagation, rupture, collapse, and the transition from active change to stability or resolution.

2.3 Documentation Layer

The documentation layer ensures that all records are captured under repeatable visual and descriptive conditions. This includes standardised imaging, fixed compositional rules, neutral colour calibration, and structured metadata.

Each recorded object is treated as a Material Record (MR).

3. Canonical Process

3.1 Material Selection

Material is selected and identified as precisely as possible. Species is recorded where known. Origin is documented geographically and, where relevant, contextually, for example whether the timber came from urban removal, storm fall, estate management, or found material.

Initial condition is also recorded, including whether the wood is green, partially dried, reclaimed, decayed, or otherwise altered before turning.

3.2 Initial Conditions

Before turning, the material is assessed for conditions likely to influence behaviour. These include moisture content, grain direction, structural irregularities, visible defects, and the presence of stress concentrations such as pith or crotch junctions.

Moisture content is measured at the base where possible and logged as the initial moisture value.

3.3 Turning Strategy

The turning process is not intended to neutralise future movement. It is used to establish conditions under which material behaviour can become visible.

Form is defined within a limited typological range such as vessel, plate, or bowl. Wall thickness may be reduced to amplify movement or varied to introduce imbalance. Tooling decisions are not merely technical but part of the recording protocol, since they influence thickness distribution, surface behaviour, and stress response.

The objective is not formal perfection but the creation of a legible material event.

3.4 Constraint Definition

Each object is shaped within a defined set of constraints. These may include symmetry or asymmetry, orientation of grain relative to form, base mass, rim geometry, and localised thinness.

These constraints affect how the object dries, distorts, fractures, ruptures, or collapses. In MRS, form is therefore understood as an instrument for exposing material behaviour rather than as a resolved end in itself.

3.5 Post-Turning Handling

After turning, the object is generally left without stabilisation unless stabilisation itself is part of the experiment. Drying may be partially controlled, loosely managed, or left open to environmental exposure, but any intervention is recorded.

Examples of interventions include wax application, oiling, wrapping, scorching, delayed sanding, or other material decisions intended to alter moisture migration or surface response.

4. Failure Taxonomy

Failure is defined as a structural response to internal stress exceeding material tolerance.

Each Material Record is classified using the following system.

Class A — Deformation

Warping, twisting, ovalisation, or other dimensional change without fibre separation.

Class B — Fracture

Crack initiation and propagation with fibre separation, while the object retains overall continuity.

Class C — Rupture

Structural failure resulting in loss of continuity, breakage, or major sectional separation.

Class D — Collapse

Loss of structural integrity due to compressive failure or instability, resulting in partial or total volumetric deformation.

Additional descriptors may be used where relevant, including initiation point, propagation direction, and temporal stage. For example, a fracture may be described as initiated at the pith, propagating radially, during early drying.

This taxonomy distinguishes between deformation, tensile failure, and compressive instability, enabling more consistent comparison across records.

5. Documentation Protocol

5.1 Imaging Conditions

All records are captured under controlled and repeatable imaging conditions. The purpose of imaging is not expressive interpretation but comparability across the archive.

Images are made against a neutral background with stable lighting and fixed composition. Light is diffuse in order to minimise glare while preserving surface information. The visual standard aims to reveal geometry, deformation, crack behaviour, and surface condition without aesthetic styling.

5.2 Composition Rules

The standard output is a square image format. Horizon position is fixed. Object placement is central and consistent. Perspective distortion is minimised. Camera height is selected to preserve reliable visibility of the rim and upper form.

The image is treated as a measuring surface, not as an expressive photograph.

5.3 Colour and Tonal Calibration

Colour and tonal adjustments are used only to restore neutrality and repeatability. They are not used to dramatise or beautify the object.

A white wall and neutral grey floor provide calibration anchors. The background should remain stable across records so that differences between objects remain attributable to the object itself rather than to variable imaging conditions.

5.4 Output

Each record may include a high-resolution archival master and a standardised publication version. Output formats may vary depending on workflow, but visual consistency is prioritised over stylistic treatment.

7. State Logic

MRS treats each object as temporally active until observation suggests otherwise.

Active

The object is undergoing visible or expected transformation.

Stable

No significant change is currently observed, though future change remains materially possible.

Resolved

The primary transformation event has concluded, whether through stabilisation, rupture, collapse, or completed deformation.

This state logic allows the archive to represent not only what an object is, but where it sits within its behavioural timeline.

8. Temporal Observation

The Material Recording System does not treat the first capture as definitive. An object may be recorded repeatedly across its evolution.

Repeated observation is especially relevant where drying continues over months, or where structural change emerges after an apparently stable period. Temporal recording may therefore include shifts in moisture content, form distortion, crack length, surface opening, collapse, or changes in perceived equilibrium.

Not all records need to be equally complete. Partial records remain valid provided that the available data is internally consistent.

9. Limits of the System

MRS is not designed as a predictive scientific model. It does not control all environmental variables and does not claim full repeatability in the strict laboratory sense.

Wood remains highly variable across species, tree, board, cut, and local condition. Moisture gradients, hidden defects, and environmental fluctuation introduce behaviours that cannot be fully standardised.

The system therefore aims for disciplined observation rather than universal prediction. Its value lies in the accumulation of comparable records over time.

10. Positioning

The Material Recording System operates between craft, material science, sculpture, and archival practice.

It is not a finishing method, nor a decorative philosophy, nor a conventional catalogue. It is a framework for documenting how material behaves when subjected to form, reduction, exposure, and time.

Within Studio Aprile, the system provides both a working method and a public structure of evidence. Individual objects can therefore be understood not only as singular works but as entries within a larger material investigation.
